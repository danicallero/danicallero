A year ago I was on the other side of this event. I came to the third edition of HackUDC as a participant, and learned a ton. This year I jumped the fence and helped organize the whole thing, the biggest hackathon in Galicia (and second largest on-site student hackathon in Spain!), seen from the inside.

Here's the first thing nobody tells you: when you help run an event for around 500 people, you end up wearing every hat there is. Support, logistics, design, whatever the day throws at you. And the irony is that I wrote far more code as an organizer than I would as a hacker.

## The work

Over the last six months I had my hands in a bit of everything:

- Contributed to the [official event website](/projects/hackudc-2026).
- Helped design of the event's graphical materials, from the accreditations to the website and social media graphics, and and found vendors to produce some of them.
- Helped with the event's social media scheduled posts using Buffer.
- Set up and managed GPUL's stripe, Apple Developer and Google Play accounts.
- Built the [operations app](/projects/hackudc-app) in React Native that staff used to run check-ins, meal passes and attendance from their phones, right there on the event floor.
- Wrote [pkpassBuilder](/projects/pkpassbuilder) to generate the signed Apple Wallet passes we sent as event tickets.

But the one that nearly made me quit, and the one I'm proudest of, was [FastTrack](/projects/fasttrack).

## Three nights

Every hackathon has the same Sunday problem. A hundred-plus teams all need to present to the judges, nobody knows when their turn is, and you spend hours in limbo waiting to be called. Anyone who has been to a hackathon knows exactly how chaotic that gets.

So during the event, I locked myself away and started building FastTrack: a queue and evaluation system to coordinate 120 projects across 12 rooms, all running at once.

I'll be the first to admit it's trench code. It would not survive an architecture review. But there is a very particular kind of adrenaline in knowing the last commit landed after a full night without sleep, thirty minutes before I generated Sunday's presentation queue, and that the thing held steady under more than 500 concurrent users without falling over.

Seeing a bit of order in the middle of that much chaos made every lost hour of sleep worth it.

## What actually stays with you

Standing in a packed auditorium, hearing the feedback from participants and from the authorities we brought in, is when the scale of it finally lands. HackUDC started as the madness of a small group of people, with not even 100 participants, and it is now 4 years later a reference point for the whole region, with almost 450 hackers. Between participants, staff, and sponsor reps, we've filled the university's auditorium to capacity, let that sink in.

That's why the code was never the point. The people were.

I got to spend six months with a team of beautiful lunatics, and I genuinely don't have a better word for them. Watching people pour themselves into assembling 500 welcome packs, or putting out logistical fires at three in the morning, is when it stops feeling like a team and starts feeling like a family.

I'm humbled, and extremely grateful to have had the opportunity to organize this, and to spend all those months with a team of such talented individuals. I learnt more about tech, logistics, org, deployments, legal, economics... in these months than I did in my first two years of CS studies.

When I tell people outside this world about the ridiculous amount of hours, and about the fact that none of it was paid, some of them think I've lost it. They can't see why anyone would do this for free. Honestly, I just think they're just missing the point, because you can't put a price on what we actually got out of it.

Thank you for letting me be part of the madness. If you want a glimpse of the weekend, there's an [official photo gallery](https://cloud.gpul.org/s/XyfQ2eGzLiJfRx4).

I can't wait to see what we build next year.
